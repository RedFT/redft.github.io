## [What My Browser History Says About Me](#what-my-browser-history-says-about-me)

I did something weird recently. I opened my browser history SQLite database and started poking around. Not for anything important, just curiosity. I wanted to see what the data said about me without the stories I tell myself.

What I found was pretty funny:

- 770 visits to a game design document
- 451 translations from Korean
- 236 trips to LinkedIn
- Hundreds of unread emails I keep meaning to clean out
- A recipe for dumplings
- A camera bag bought before the camera

<a href="https://images.pexels.com/photos/14900002/pexels-photo-14900002.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb" target="_blank"><img src="https://images.pexels.com/photos/14900002/pexels-photo-14900002.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=800" alt="A man laughing at his phone as if reading a ridiculous translation" style="width:75%;border-radius:8px;margin:1.5rem auto;display:block;aspect-ratio:1/1;object-fit:cover;" /></a>

Looking at it all together, a few patterns jumped out. I start a lot of things and finish some of them. I research trips more than I take them. I learn Korean by accident and I really like studying game lore. None of this was surprising, but seeing it all in one place made it feel more real than my own memory ever does.

That is the main thing I got out of this. My memory is generous. It tells me I read more philosophy than I do and that I am further along in Korean than I actually am. The browser history is less flattering but more honest. It remembers every course I abandoned and every recipe I meant to cook but never did.

<a href="https://images.pexels.com/photos/17483868/pexels-photo-17483868.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb" target="_blank"><img src="https://images.pexels.com/photos/17483868/pexels-photo-17483868.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=800" alt="A glowing purple wireframe illustration of a brain" style="width:75%;border-radius:8px;margin:1.5rem auto;display:block;" /></a>

But here is the thing about all those open tabs. They are not failures. They are evidence of curiosity, of a brain that still wants to learn things. The philosophy course I never finished. The Korean words I looked up and forgot. The dive trip I researched instead of booking. The game design document I kept coming back to. Each one is a moment where I wanted to learn something, try something, be someone who does that thing.

I am still going to open too many tabs. I am still going to start more projects than I finish. But after reading my own browser history, I realized the curiosity is the point, not the completion. The browser history is not a report card. It is a map of everything I have ever wondered about. And I would rather be someone with a thousand half-pulled threads than someone who stopped pulling altogether.
